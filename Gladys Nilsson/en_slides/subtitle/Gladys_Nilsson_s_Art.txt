Section 2, Celebrating Small Everyday Things

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0006.png]
So you might be wondering where exactly does this unique perspective come from?
While born in 1940 to Swedish immigrants on Chicago's north side,
Nilsson grew up in a very grounded, working-class environment.
While a lot of other artists were out there hunting for grand historical narratives,
she just ignored all of that.
From a young age, she was totally obsessed with quirky everyday details,
like a neighbor's waving arm, or the distorted reflection in a corner grocery store window.
By 1958, that hyper-observant eye landed her a spot as a full-time student
at the prestigious School of the Art Institute of Chicago.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0007.png]
Actually, her entire philosophy is perfectly summed up in this one brilliant confession.
I like to celebrate small things.
Acting as sort of a self-proclaimed voyeur,
Nilsson magnifies tiny, awkward human gestures.
A clumsy step, an overextended stretch, a weird glance.
She takes these totally trivial moments and transforms them into this dense, magnificent visual theater.

Moving right along to Section 3, The Layering Alchemy of Watercolor

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0009.png]
But how exactly does she pull off this incredibly dense theater?
It actually comes down to a pivotal constraint that completely revolutionized her medium, by accident.
Back in 1962, a pregnancy forced a major pivot.
To protect her health from the toxic turpentine fumes of oil painting, she picked up watercolors.
And what started as a necessary compromise quickly turned into an absolute lifelong obsession
with the fluid, unpredictable nature of water and pigment.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0010.png]
She essentially threw the watercolor rulebook right out the window.
Here's her unique layering alchemy.
Step one, she draws this highly precise dense pencil scaffolding.
Think of it like the structural bones of a building.
Step two, she layers transparent watercolor washes over that structure, wash after wash.
The result?
It's an oil-like, heavy, luminous surface that miraculously keeps its watercolor transparency.
It's really incredible.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0011.png]
You can see her absolute mastery of this technique in early works like Kold Toddiebears from 1970.
I mean, there is literally zero breathing room here.
The transparent layers create this mesmerizing, shimmering effect over that invisible pencil structure,
proving once and for all that watercolor absolutely does not have to be faint or fragile.

Section four, Hairy Who and Lowbrow culture.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0013.png]
Now, Nilsson didn't just develop this style in a vacuum.
She was a key part of this rebellious 1960s Chicago art movement known as the Hairy Who.
Formed in 1965 with five other grads, including her husband Jim Nutt, they were total renegades.
While the New York art world was obsessing over sterile, ultra-serious minimalism,
this Chicago crew was weaponizing Lowbrow visual culture.
They embraced comic books, tattoo art, and the flashy kind of vulgar imagery of pinball machines.
To them, art wasn't some quiet church.
It was a loud, colorful, in-your-face carnival.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0014.png]
And you can completely feel that carnival spirit taking over in her 1971 painting
Dipped Dick: Adam and Eve After Cranach.
She playfully takes a classical, very serious religious motif
and just inverts it into a chaotic, secular comedy.
Filtered through her densely packed, humorous lens,
the canvas is filled to the absolute brim with wriggling life and cartoonish exaggeration.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0015.png]
Next up, section five, Making Women Bigger.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0016.png]
This audacious style directly fueled her evolving gender politics.
In 1973, Nilsson made history as one of the first female artists
to ever land a solo exhibition at the Whitney Museum.
From that point on, her depiction of the female form
became this radical, feminist manifesto.
The older I get, the bigger I want these women to be.
In a culture that was constantly trying to shrink or domesticate women,
Nilsson flat-out refused.
She insisted on making her women massive,
expanding them until they literally spilled over the edges of the canvas.

[Gladys_Nilsson_s_Art_slide_0017.png]
Just look at that philosophy in action in Beset by Small Problems from 1990.
This massive female figure just utterly dominates the composition.
Sure, she's swarmed by these tiny, bug-like issues,
the trivial, annoying daily problems we all deal with,
but she remains the undeniable center of gravity.
She is large, completely unbothered, and entirely in command of her space.
