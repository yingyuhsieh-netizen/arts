What if the Renaissance did not begin with a single Florentine hero? Travel to medieval Rome and meet Pietro Cavallini, the overlooked master whose experiments with light, volume, space, and human emotion helped transform Italian art before Giotto’s celebrated Padua cycle.

From the frescoes painted for St. Paul Outside the Walls around 1277–1285—later destroyed by fire—to the luminous mosaics of Santa Maria in Trastevere, Cavallini developed a distinctly Roman naturalism rooted in the city’s classical and early Christian heritage. His apostles possess weight and bone structure; his architecture recedes into believable space; even the infant Christ twists with the restless energy of a real child.

The story reaches its dramatic turning point at Santa Cecilia in Trastevere, where Cavallini’s *Last Judgment* was covered with Baroque plaster in 1724 and rediscovered in 1900. Its return forced historians to reconsider Giorgio Vasari’s Florence-centered account and Cavallini’s relationship to Giotto.
