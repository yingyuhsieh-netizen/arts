How did a Renaissance painting once known mainly to a small circle become one of the most recognizable images in Western art? Follow the extraordinary journey of Sandro Botticelli’s *The Birth of Venus* from fifteenth-century Florence to its sensational international debut in London.

The story turns on the 1930 exhibition *Italian Art 1200–1900* at Burlington House. More than 500,000 visitors attended during its three-month run, and Botticelli’s canvas left Tuscany for the first time. Behind that triumph was a calculated display of cultural prestige shaped by Benito Mussolini’s Fascist government.

We also look closely at the painting itself: Hesiod’s violent creation myth transformed through Agnolo Poliziano’s poetry; Zephyrus, Chloris, and Hora guiding Venus toward shore; the classical *Venus pudica* pose and contrapposto stance; and Botticelli’s unusual choice of lightweight canvas. The painting’s Medici associations and Neoplatonic meanings are compelling, although its original patron and destination remain uncertain.

From years of neglect at the Uffizi to nineteenth-century revival, Hollywood echoes in *Dr. No*, and contemporary reworkings of Western beauty ideals, this is the story of an image that every era has made its own.
